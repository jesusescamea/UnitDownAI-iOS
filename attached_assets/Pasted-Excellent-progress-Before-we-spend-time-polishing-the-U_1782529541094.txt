Excellent progress. Before we spend time polishing the UI, I want to validate the workflow itself.

Please do not focus yet on card density, colors, spacing, FAB placement, or visual polish.

Instead, I want to evaluate whether this truly matches a commercial HVAC technician's real workflow.

As we review the merged mockup, use these design principles:

## 1. Every tap must save time.

If a screen, dialog, or interaction doesn't make the technician faster, simplify or remove it.

---

## 2. The technician should rarely type.

The preferred order of data capture should be:

Photo

Voice

Measurement

One-tap selection

Typing only as a last resort.

---

## 3. The timeline is the product.

Everything should become a chronological event.

No duplicate screens.

No separate documentation workflow.

The report should build itself naturally from the technician's work.

---

## 4. The technician should never wonder what to do next.

The interface should gently guide the next logical step.

Examples:

• Belt replaced → suggest belt size and pulley information.

• Capacitor replaced → suggest capacitance and voltage.

• Motor replaced → suggest amps and rotation.

The app should quietly coach documentation without interrupting work.

---

## 5. Everything should be recoverable.

If the technician loses signal, closes the app, switches to another app, answers a phone call, or locks the phone, the job should reopen exactly where it was.

Nothing should ever be lost.

---

## 6. Photos should become structured information.

Instead of simply attaching photos, they should automatically become:

Nameplate

Failed Part

Repair

Wiring

Measurement

Overall Unit

The technician can override the category if needed.

---

## 7. The interface should reduce cognitive load.

At any moment, the technician should only think about the next action—not the final report.

The report should assemble itself automatically in the background.

---

## 8. The final experience should feel rewarding.

Completing a job should feel like generating an official maintenance record, not simply pressing "Finish."

The technician should feel that they have created something valuable for themselves, their office, the customer, and the equipment's future history.

---
